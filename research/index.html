Research

Below is a synopsis of my research interests, with a more detailed list of possible research topics and directions for potential students and collaborators on the topics page (30/12/14: empty for now, will be added to over the coming weeks). My publications are listed and many talks are available for download on my publications page. Please visit the projects page if you are interested in seeing visualisations of my work.

For the moment I'm going to keep this quite brief, but I will either extend or refine the writing later.

My main field of research is statistical mechanics, which is the study of systems with many, many constituents, with the goal of understanding how these constituents can cooperate to bring about global changes of state, otherwise known as phase transitions. Typical examples of phase transitions are liquid water freezing, or boiling, or a magnet losing its magnetic field when it's heated.

In particular, I study various mathematical models of statistical mechanical systems, and exploit the fundamental property of universality. Universality guarantees that no matter how simple or idealised a model is, provided it is in the same “universality class” the model will have exactly the same fundamental properties as real, physical systems. One example of universality, and in fact the one I'm most familiar with, is the way self-avoiding walks exactly model certain aspects of polymers (long molecules) in a solvent.

For my PhD I worked with Barry McCoy at Stony Brook and made some improvements in the numerical and analytical calculation of virial coefficients for hard spheres. Hard spheres are an excellent model of colloids and are historically important for a host of reasons, including the fact that when compressed the hard sphere system change state from a fluid to a solid. However, much remains to be done in this direction!

More recently my primary research focus has been to build upon the intuition I have gained into the efficient Monte Carlo simulation of self-avoiding walks via the pivot algorithm to a wide variety of other problems, both in the field of statistical mechanics and more broadly. I believe that significant progress is possible for a wide range of applications where Markov chain Monte Carlo is the state of the art.

I'm interested in many other topics (it may be shorter to list things I find uninteresting!), but in particular I have research interests in developing efficient enumeration algorithms, physical combinatorics, climate change (in particular policy around methane and carbon dioxide, so-called multi-gas modelling), modelling more broadly, polymer physics, and mathematical visualisation.
